\section{Introduction}

Wireless access networks have traditionally been designed under the assumption of static infrastructure and slowly varying demand. Even in advanced multi-antenna systems such as massive MIMO \cite{fundamentalsmimo,mimofornextgen} and \gls{cf} architectures \cite{Interdonato_Bjornson_Quoc_Ngo_Frenger_Larsson_2019,cellfreemimobook}, the physical deployment of access points (\glspl{ap}) is typically fixed, while adaptability is achieved through signal processing, scheduling, and beamforming. Although these approaches significantly improve spectral efficiency and coverage, they inherently assume that spatial mismatches between infrastructure and users can be mitigated purely at the signal level.

Recent research has shown that bringing antennas closer to users through distributed deployments improves both energy and spectral efficiency, but also makes performance strongly dependent on the actual placement of the distributed infrastructure \cite{9645048,loschenbrand_towards_2022,zhu2022applacement}. This shifts part of the design problem from signal processing to geometry. In parallel, realistic channel modeling and measurement campaigns have enabled more faithful evaluation of wireless systems in dynamic environments. Geometry-based stochastic channel models such as QuaDRiGa \cite{6758357} and measurement-driven studies in mobile and vehicular scenarios \cite{6981979,8809274,8963325} provide detailed insight into spatial and temporal channel dynamics. More recently, differentiable and site-specific ray-tracing tools such as Sionna RT have made it possible to evaluate radio systems directly in detailed three-dimensional scenes, bridging the gap between abstract channel models and environment-aware wireless design \cite{sionna_rt_2023}. However, despite these advances, most existing works still treat infrastructure placement as either static or offline-optimized, rather than as a variable that can be repeatedly adapted over time.

At the same time, the evolution toward 6G systems emphasizes extreme densification, distributed intelligence, and tight integration between communication, sensing, and control \cite{8869705,9390169,9397776}. Concepts such as cell-free networking, radio stripes, and distributed antenna systems aim to provide ubiquitous service by leveraging spatial diversity and user-centric operation \cite{9145164,Forenza_Perlman_2015}. As this line of work matures, mobility is becoming a central challenge. In particular, recent work on mobile cell-free massive MIMO highlights that user mobility fundamentally affects channel acquisition, coordination, scalability, and practical deployment decisions, and therefore requires new architectures and control strategies beyond static infrastructure assumptions \cite{zheng2024mobilecfmimo}. Yet, even in that context, adaptation is still predominantly considered at the logical or signal-processing level, rather than through physically reconfigurable low-height infrastructure.

The Cocoon project departs from these conventions by introducing a fluid networking paradigm, in which infrastructure itself becomes part of the adaptive loop. Instead of optimizing only transmission strategies, the network can reconfigure its physical topology by repositioning low-height \glspl{ap} based on measured channel conditions. This concept builds on recent advances in experimental testbeds and open frameworks for 6G research \cite{9815696,simulator,Call2311:Open}, while extending the state of the art toward infrastructure-aware adaptation in realistic urban environments.

The Cocoon project targets a wireless access paradigm in which the network adapts to the fluidity of the city rather than assuming a static infrastructure and a stationary demand profile. The motivating hypothesis from the project proposal is that a practical \gls{cf} network becomes more feasible when low-height infrastructure can be co-created, repositioned, and activated only when the measured channel conditions justify it. In that view, the citizens are not only traffic sources but also anchors for sensing, mounting, and reconfiguring the surrounding network.

This article narrows that broader vision to a simulation-based study of low-height infrastructure adaptation. The implemented framework covers wall-mounted candidate \glspl{ap}, outdoor user mobility, path-based \gls{csi} extraction, and repeated placement decisions over relocation windows in an urban scene. By combining distributed-MIMO evaluation with site-specific ray tracing and mobility-aware infrastructure reselection, the work positions itself at the intersection of adaptive cell-free networking, realistic radio-environment modeling, and practical \gls{ap} placement research \cite{zheng2024mobilecfmimo,sionna_rt_2023,zhu2022applacement}. Within this scope, the work focuses on evaluating how infrastructure mobility interacts with channel-aware decision-making. In particular, it considers distributed low-height deployments, a centralized rooftop proxy, and relocation-capable strategies that exploit proxy-based channel knowledge to guide \gls{ap} placement. The emphasis is not on full-stack network operation, but on isolating the geometric and physical-layer effects of adaptive infrastructure under realistic mobility and propagation conditions.

The main contributions of the present study are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item a concise technical framing of Cocoon as a low-height, movable-\gls{ap}, citizen-co-created access-network concept,
    \item a reproducible description of the Rabot outdoor simulation pipeline implemented in \texttt{COCOON-Sionna}, and
    \item a common analysis workflow for geometry, \gls{sinr}, \gls{esr}, and relocation-cost results derived from exported simulation artifacts.
\end{itemize}
