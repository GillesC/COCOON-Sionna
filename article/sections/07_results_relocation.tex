\label{sec:results_relocation}

\Cref{fig:schedule_pair} quantifies the movement cost associated with movable infrastructure. As expected, \strategy1[title] and \strategy2[title] induce no physical motion. Among the proxy-based movable controllers, \strategy4[title] reaches a move fraction of \SI{80.0}{\percent} and a total traveled distance of \SI{14.2}{km}, with a mean per-move distance of \SI{49.2}{m}. \strategy5[title] induces more motion, with \num{311} moves, a move fraction of \SI{86.4}{\percent}, a total traveled distance of \SI{18.6}{km}, and a mean move distance of \SI{59.9}{m}.

This ranking is important when combined with the radio metrics from \cref{sec:results_sinr}. \strategy4[title] not only outperforms \strategy5[title] in mean and lower-tail \gls{sinr}/\gls{esr}, it also requires less motion: about \SI{24}{\percent} less total relocation distance and a smaller move fraction. Thus, within the two nearest-user proxy controllers, the phase-aware proxy-\gls{esr} rule dominates the power-only proxy rule in both radio performance and movement cost for the present scenario. \strategy2[title] therefore remains the practical zero-mobility reference, while \strategy4[title] defines the best radio-performance point among the movable strategies.

The relocation-distance \gls{cdf} adds one further observation: the movable controllers do not differ only in how often they move, but also in how broadly they explore the candidate pool. \strategy4[title] uses \num{140} distinct positions over the run, while \strategy5[title] uses \num{162}. Hence, the power-only rule is not merely more active; it is also spatially less conservative. In this dataset, that additional exploration does not translate into better radio performance.
