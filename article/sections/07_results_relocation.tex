\label{sec:results_relocation}

\Cref{fig:schedule_pair} quantifies the price paid for movable infrastructure. As expected, \texttt{central\_massive\_mimo} and \texttt{distributed\_fixed} induce no physical motion. Among the proxy-based movable controllers, optimization~2 reaches a move fraction of \SI{80.0}{\percent} and a total traveled distance of \SI{14.2}{km}, with a mean per-move distance of \SI{49.2}{m}. Optimization~3 is the more aggressive controller, with 311 moves, a move fraction of \SI{86.4}{\percent}, a total traveled distance of \SI{18.6}{km}, and a mean move distance of \SI{59.9}{m}.

This ranking is important when combined with the radio metrics from \cref{sec:results_sinr}. Optimization~2 not only outperforms optimization~3 in mean and lower-tail \gls{sinr}/\gls{esr}, it also requires less motion: about \SI{24}{\percent} less total relocation distance and a smaller move fraction. Thus, within the two nearest-user proxy controllers, the phase-aware proxy-\gls{esr} rule is strictly preferable to the power-only proxy rule in the present scenario. The fixed distributed baseline therefore remains the practical zero-mobility reference, while optimization~2 defines the best radio-performance point among the movable strategies.

The relocation-distance CDF adds one further observation: the movable controllers do not differ only in how often they move, but also in how broadly they explore the candidate pool. Optimization~2 uses 140 distinct positions over the run, while optimization~3 uses 162. Hence, the power-only rule is not merely more active; it is also spatially less conservative. In this dataset, that additional exploration does not translate into better radio performance.
