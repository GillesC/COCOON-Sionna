\begin{abstract}
This paper studies low-height distributed-\gls{mimo} access networks with repositionable access points in an outdoor urban setting. A site-specific simulation campaign constructs wall-mounted candidate \glspl{ap}, simulates pedestrian and bicycle mobility, computes path-based \gls{csi}, and compares a central rooftop proxy, a fixed distributed deployment, and two proxy-based relocation-capable distributed deployments over a common scenario. The study formalizes the \gls{sinr}- and \gls{esr}-based evaluation model, a nearest-user \gls{ue}-to-\gls{ue} proxy-\gls{csi} heuristic that evaluates a proxy-\gls{esr} objective from wideband proxy channels, and a nearest-user proxy-power heuristic that ranks candidate subsets from stored \gls{ue}-to-\gls{ue} link-power measurements scaled to the \gls{ap} transmit-power budget. In the Rabot scenario, the phase-aware proxy-\gls{csi} controller is the strongest strategy: it reaches a mean \gls{esr} of \SI{133.1}{bit/s/Hz}, improves the \num{10}th-percentile \gls{esr} by \SI{88.8}{\percent} over the fixed distributed baseline, and reduces the \SI{0}{dB} outage fraction from \SI{4.41}{\percent} to \SI{1.87}{\percent}. The power-only proxy controller is second-best in radio performance but requires more motion. Relocation effort is quantified through schedule-level displacement statistics, allowing radio-performance gains to be interpreted together with physical reconfiguration cost.
\end{abstract}
