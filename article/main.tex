\begin{abstract}
This paper studies low-height distributed-\gls{mimo} access networks with repositionable access points in an outdoor urban setting. Using the \texttt{COCOON-Sionna} framework, the workflow generates wall-mounted candidate \glspl{ap}, simulates pedestrian and bicycle mobility, computes path-based \gls{csi}, and compares a central rooftop proxy, a fixed distributed deployment, and three relocation-capable distributed deployments over a common scenario. The article formalizes the \gls{sinr}- and \gls{esr}-based evaluation model, a history-aware $K$-nearest relocation heuristic that scores exponentially aged best-user deployed-\gls{ap}-to-\gls{ue} \gls{sinr} samples, a nearest-user \gls{ue}-to-\gls{ue} proxy-\gls{csi} heuristic that evaluates a proxy-\gls{esr} objective from wideband proxy channels, and a nearest-user proxy-power heuristic that ranks candidate subsets from stored \gls{ue}-to-\gls{ue} link-power measurements scaled to the \gls{ap} transmit-power budget. In the Rabot scenario, the proxy-\gls{esr} controller is the strongest strategy: it reaches a mean \gls{esr} of \SI{133.1}{bit/s/Hz}, improves the 10th-percentile \gls{esr} by \SI{88.8}{\percent} over the fixed distributed baseline, and reduces the \SI{0}{dB} outage fraction from \SI{4.41}{\percent} to \SI{1.87}{\percent}. The power-only proxy controller is second-best in radio performance but requires more motion, whereas the historical local-\gls{csi} controller improves average \gls{esr} over the static baseline while substantially degrading the lower-tail \gls{sinr}. The paper also documents the postprocessing workflow that produces geometry, performance, and relocation-cost figures from reproducible simulation outputs.
\end{abstract}
